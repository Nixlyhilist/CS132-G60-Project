Research question

Was there a noticeable increase of misinformation tweets about mental health during election seasons? (2016, 2022 Presidential Elections; 2019 Senatorial Elections)

Hypothesis

There was a noticeable increase of tweets that spread misinformation about mental health during the election seasons.

Null hypothesis

There was no noticeable increase of tweets that spread misinformation about mental health during the election seasons.

Action Plan

Analyze the volume of misinformation tweets about mental health from 2016-2022.

We mined the internet for fake news data.

Here's what we've gathered and observed.

This dataset contains 102 tweets taken from 2016-2022. Let's call this dataset "df"

The columns are as follows:

  • Keywords - what was searched on Twitter to find the tweet
  • Account handle
  • Account name
  • Account bio
  • Account handle
  • Account type - whether the Twitter user is Anonymous or Identified
  • Joined - when their account was created
  • Following
  • Followers
  • Location
  • Tweet
  • Date posted
  • Likes
  • Replies
  • Retweets

Data pre-processing

During exploration, it was discovered that only "Account bio" and "Location" contained null values.

Since these columns didn't hold a particularly significant value in exploring the data,
the null values were simply replaced with signifiers that explicitly said their state.





The "Joined" and "Date posted" columns contained datetime data types that were varying in formats.
For the purposes of this exploration, their values were changed into string data type following a mm/yy format.


Another column was introduced, taking the sum of "Likes", "Replies", and "Retweets", to form the column "Interactions"





A new dataset

For ease of exploration tweet frequency, a new dataset, which we'll call "date_df" was created with the following columns:

  • Month - this is just a list of the months from 2016 - 2022 in mm/yy format
  • Count - this counts the number of tweets that were found for every month listed
  • Interactions - this counts the number of interactions that tweets received each month for every month listed

Let's take a look at our data

Let's talk about our data science methodology.

To test our hypothesis, we had to look at the data of the frequency of monthly tweets
from January 2016 to December 2022.





For a visual, the image above shows the mean tweets per month during the election periods, compared againsts the rest of the months. As an example, the 2016 election period, spanning from March 2016 to July 2016, is compared to the rest of the months from 2016 to 2022. This goes the same for the 2019 election period, and the 2022 election period. The "combined" section shows the mean tweets per month for all three election periods combined, compared the mean tweets per month of the rest of the months.



Our team landed on the Wilcoxon rank-sum test as our choice for the statistical analysis method.


The Wilcoxon rank-sum test fits aptly because the data that we wanted to look at is not normalized,
as seen from the line graph in the previous section.

Here's what we found out.



After applying the Wilcoxon rank-sum test on the four comparisons that we wanted to check, it turns out that all of them had a p-value greater than 0.05, as visualized by the image above.

So in conclusion, we can say that our null-hypothesis has not been successfully rejected. In terms of what we want to know for this project, unfortunately, that means we cannot confidently say that the election periods cause an insurgence of mental health misinformation tweets.
